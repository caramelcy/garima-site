Doctoral research · 2018 — 2026

Bonds under force,
from theory to electrons

Pull hard enough on almost any bond and it breaks sooner. A few bonds do the opposite — they grip harder the harder you pull. My thesis is about the physics of that, worked out in theory and simulation.

The question

Why would a bond get stronger when you pull it?

Force does work along the escape path, so it normally lowers the barrier a bond must climb to break — pull harder, break sooner. That's a slip bond, and it's what nearly everything does.

A catch bond inverts it. Over some range of force, its lifetime rises as tension grows. Nature uses this where things must hold on precisely when they're being torn away — bacteria gripping tissue against flow, cells resisting shear. Understanding it means understanding a bond whose energy landscape reshapes under the very force trying to break it.

My work treats this at the level of a polymer: not one bond in isolation, but a chain where the load is shared, redistributed, and where broken bonds can re-form. That coupling — many bonds, one force, rupture and rebinding at once — is where the analytical difficulty and the interesting physics both live.

Approach

Four ways at the same problem

01 / MODEL

Bead–spring polymers

Coarse-grained chains whose bonds break and re-form with force-dependent rates, capturing catch and slip kinetics without simulating every atom.

02 / STOCHASTIC

Simulation

Langevin, Brownian, and Monte Carlo dynamics — rupture is a random event, so results are averaged over roughly 1,000 independent trajectories before anything is believed.

03 / ANALYTICAL

Fokker–Planck & master equations

Mean-field formalism for the bound-fraction dynamics, and analytical solutions for strongly nonlinear coupled PDEs via the Homotopy Analysis Method and a travelling-wave treatment.

04 / NUMERICS

Solvers in C & Mathematica

Fourth-order Runge–Kutta with adaptive step size, written in C, for rupture-front velocity analysis; Mathematica for the symbolic and semi-analytical work.

Current direction

From coarse-grained
to all-atom, and to electrons

The theory says rupture force should depend on how fast you load a bond — logarithmically, not linearly. I've been testing that directly with steered molecular dynamics: build a solvated all-atom system from a crystal structure, align the pulling axis to the ligand's real exit path, then pull with a moving harmonic spring and record the force it transmits.

Because rupture is stochastic, one pull means nothing. The pipeline runs replicated trajectories at several loading rates, extracts a rupture-force distribution at each, and fits the loading-rate dependence to recover the bond's kinetic parameters. Every stage is verified before the next is trusted — which is how the interesting failures get caught rather than published.

Beyond that sits the question I find hardest and most interesting: when a bond is strained, does its electronic structure change in a way that geometry alone doesn't account for? That's a quantum-chemistry problem — QTAIM topology of the electron density, natural bond orbital charge transfer — and it's where this work is heading.
